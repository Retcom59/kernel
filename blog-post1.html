Commercial drone deliveries are growing



Research by McKinsey shows that commercial drone deliveries have started their high climb, fuelled by the pandemic and show no signs of slowing. The industry has gone from a mere 8000 deliveries in 2018 (Amazons planned launch date) to a forecasted 1.4 million deliveries this year. The annual growth rate from 2021 to 2022 has more than doubled to 192% compared to an average growth rate of 81% from the previous 4 years.

Most of todays drone delivery applications have been used for delivering medical supplies, but this is quickly changing with the emergence of players such as Wing, Amazon and Manna who are focussing on direct to consumer deliveries. At Venda Drone Delivery we believe there will be a paradigm shift with quick commerce grocery delivery apps coming to the realisation that their unprofitable business models are unsustainable and drone delivery will be ready to change that.

A question often posed to us is whether we have the infrastructure & resources to compete with giants such as Amazon or Wing (acquired by Google). Of course not. However, we don't have to because almost every player in the drone delivery space is going about this differently.

A few examples:

Amazon Prime Drones plan to fulfil its own products, possibly using patented bee-hive like fulfilment hubs. An ideal solution for urban areas, provided the e-commerce giant can get through certain regulatory and safety hurdles.

Wing aims to work with local businesses as an additional delivery option for consumers. Actively delivering in Australia, Finland and the U.S., Wing have demonstrated clear demand for their service and the technology seems to be working well. Wing creates large bases where its drones are on standby, ready to pick up orders from partner businesses and deliver them right to the customers doorstep.

Matternet works with government and private companies with a primary focus on making medical deliveries with their quadcopter drone. Without a doubt, the COVID-19 pandemic has boosted their growth within the space. They also have a unique approach with their flower shaped Matternet station for point A to B deliveries.

Wingcopter seems to be building a fleet of pricy VTOL (vertical takeoff and landing) drones with the ability to cover long distance journeys to remote areas. Providing a variety of solutions from medical to scientific research, they seem to be targeting niche opportunities that require integrated custom solutions. As seen with the examples above, each company has its own unique twist within this growing market and it's exciting to see where they all end up in a few years time.
